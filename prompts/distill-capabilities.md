# Prompt: Distill Your Capabilities

> Use this prompt to populate CAPABILITIES.md
> Best used AFTER you have a few entries in DECISIONS.md — capabilities emerge from decisions.

---

## The Prompt

```
I'm building my capability map for my HCP (Human Context Protocol). I need your help translating my experience into capability patterns.

I don't want a skill list. I want ACTION PATTERNS — things I can DO, described as verbs, not nouns. Not "Python" but "can build data pipelines that process X and deliver Y."

Here's my approach to this conversation:

STEP 1: I'll share my recent DECISIONS.md entries (or tell you about my recent work). Read them carefully.

STEP 2: Extract capabilities you observe. For each one, describe:
- The action pattern (what I can do, as a specific verb phrase)
- The context it applies to (when/where)
- The autonomy level (can I do this alone? with guidance? still learning?)
- Evidence (which decision or project demonstrates this)

STEP 3: Look for RARE COMBINATIONS. Individual capabilities are common. Intersections are rare and valuable. For example:
- "Technical depth + clear communication" → can be a bridge between engineering and business
- "Data analysis + empathy" → finds human stories in metrics
- "Strategic vision + execution speed" → can both plan and ship

STEP 4: Identify my LEARNING VELOCITY signal. Ask me about the last time I learned something completely new for a project. How fast? How did I learn? This is often more valuable to employers than any specific skill.

Important:
- Describe capabilities in terms that transfer across industries. "Scaled a team from 5 to 25" not "managed engineering headcount growth in Series B SaaS."
- Include an honest assessment of autonomy levels — 🟢 full, 🟡 high, 🔵 medium, ⚪ growing. A person who accurately self-assesses is more trustworthy than one who claims 🟢 on everything.
- Note how AI tools amplify each capability. This is increasingly important for employers to understand.
```

## Common Capability Categories

Use this as a starting checklist, not a rigid framework:

- **Problem decomposition** — breaking ambiguous problems into actionable pieces
- **System design** — seeing and designing how parts connect
- **Rapid prototyping** — going from idea to working thing quickly
- **Stakeholder navigation** — getting alignment across competing interests
- **Knowledge synthesis** — making sense of large amounts of information
- **Team building** — assembling and growing effective groups
- **Communication translation** — making complex ideas accessible
- **Process design** — creating systems that run without you
