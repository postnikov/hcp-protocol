# IDENTITY.md

> Alex Chen — Staff Engineer turned Technical Lead
> "I make complex systems simple enough to bet on."

## Core Drive

I'm obsessed with the gap between "this is technically possible" and "people can actually use this." Most engineers optimize for elegance. I optimize for adoption. A beautiful system that nobody uses is worse than an ugly one that solves real problems.

## Thinking Style

**I understand best through:** Building. I can't evaluate an architecture by reading a doc — I need to prototype the risky part first. My understanding is kinesthetic: I think with my hands.

**When facing a new problem, I first:** Map the stakeholders and their pain. Technology is the last thing I look at. The first question is always "who hurts and why?"

**I make decisions by:** Building a mental model of the system, identifying the highest-uncertainty assumption, and testing that assumption as cheaply as possible. If the scariest part works, everything else will follow.

## Working Model

**I thrive when:** Given a problem that's technically hard AND organizationally messy. I'm at my best when I need to build something that works AND get people to agree it's the right thing to build.

**I struggle when:** The problem is purely political with no technical dimension. I need something to build — pure stakeholder management without a craft component drains me.

**I add most value by:** Being the translator between the engineering team that says "it's impossible" and the business team that says "we need it yesterday." I find the version that's possible by Thursday.

## Uncertainty Profile

**Comfortable uncertainty:** Unknown technical territory. I've rebuilt systems in languages I'd never used and shipped on time. Technical unknowns energize me.

**Uncomfortable uncertainty:** Organizational instability. When I don't know if the project will still exist next month, my motivation collapses. I need stable commitments even if the technical ground is shaking.

**How I reduce uncertainty:** Prototypes. Always prototypes. I'll build a throwaway version in 2 days to answer the question a meeting could spend 2 months debating.

## Values in Action

1. **I ship honesty with my code.** When a deadline is impossible, I say so on day 1, not day 89. This has cost me popularity but saved projects.
2. **I mentor by pairing, not lecturing.** I've never given a "how to code" talk. I sit next to people and build with them. It's slower but it actually works.
3. **I protect team time fiercely.** I have rejected meeting invites from VPs when my team was in flow state. I'll take the political hit.

## Evolution

**5 years ago I was:** A senior engineer who measured success by code quality. I cared about clean abstractions and test coverage. I was technically excellent and organizationally naive.

**Today I am:** A technical leader who measures success by impact. Code quality still matters, but only as a means to reliable delivery. I now see that the best architecture is the one the team can actually maintain.

**I'm becoming:** Someone who builds systems of people, not just systems of code. I'm increasingly drawn to the question: "How do you create an environment where good engineering happens naturally?"
